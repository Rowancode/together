Face to Face with Refugees Fleeing War-torn Syria

There are 65.3 million people across the globe who have been forced to flee their homes. There has been a great deal of debate in the media as to what to call these individuals, and many terms have been used “migrants”, “economic migrants”, “refugees”, “asylum seekers” or even a “swarm”.

In focusing on these labels rather than the individual beneath them, too often we lose sight of the people affected by this global crisis. Hidden behind the statistics and the political debate are people who, a few years ago, had lives that looked a lot like yours or mine.

To mark World Refugee Day this year on 20 June One Young World [Maybe explain who they are] sponsored my art exhibition, The Divine Image: For Mercy Has A Human Heart, in The Crypt Gallery in London.

This exhibition collected portrait paintings of refugees I’ve met in refugee camps in Calais and Jordan, where I partnered with UN Refugee Agency and Relief International to organise art projects for Syrian refugees.

Through painting these portraits, I hoped to humanise the individuals forced to flee their homes. Too often reports of this crisis lost sight of personal stories, instead focusing on statistics to understand events. In crises of such scale, such a reaction is understandable. However, if in doing so we lose sight of the humanity we share with those fleeing their homes, we only add to a political climate that accentuates difference and fear. Such an approach can never hope to solve this human tragedy.

The change we all seek begins when we engage with people rather than with numbers. It was with this in mind that I travelled to the Refugee camps in Jordan and Calais. Here I had the privilege of hearing some of their stories.

Whilst volunteering in the Calais ‘Jungle’, I met Abdul. Abdul lost his whole family in an air strike in Syria. He made the dangerous journey to the Calais ‘Jungle’ alone and vulnerable. No fifteen year-old should ever have to endure this.

I met this brother and sister in Jordan. They took part in one of my art projects for Syrian children, painting their experiences of war. They drew tanks, soldiers, dead bodies and destroyed homes: only a small glimpse of the traumas they have faced.

This is a portrait of a Syrian woman I met in Za’atari, the largest refugee camp in Jordan home to 80,000 refugees.

In the wake of recent terrorist attacks and a divisive referendum campaign, we have almost entirely lost sight of the individuals affected by these conflicts. However, with the rise of the far right and increase in discrimination and xenophobia throughout Europe, there has never been a more important time to remember the human lives lie behind the tide of demonizing rhetoric.

We must remember that these individuals have been compelled to flee conflict, persecution and human rights abuses; they are not to be dismissed as economic opportunists or a terrorist threat.

In each of my refugee portrait paintings, the gaze is direct. Perhaps because, in meeting someone face to face, we encounter their humanity and we can no longer objectify them as a "Migrant" or diminish them as a "swarm". We encounter them as an equal.

Nothing is more important than empathy for another human being's suffering. We have to feel compassion for one another if we are going to survive with dignity. Ultimately it is not only those in Syria whose humanity is at risk. In failing to recognise their humanity we call into question our own.

In the coming months Together will publish on what practical steps can be taken to help those in both Calais and Syria. We will also look to understand why people have been so hostile to those caught in this crises, laying out a plan to resolve the social issues we suspect to be at the root of this.

Author: Hannah Thomas
